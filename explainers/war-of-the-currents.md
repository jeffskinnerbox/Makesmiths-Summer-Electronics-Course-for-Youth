# The War of the Currents
Nikola Tesla and Thomas Edison both played key roles in the "War of the Currents."

What is the answer to these questions:

1. What did each of them — Edison, Tesla, and Westinghouse — actually do?
1. Why did George Westinghouse and Nikola Tesla together win this war?

The "War of the Currents" was a roughly decade-long fight in the 1880s and 1890s over *how* the United States
would be wired for electricity — with **direct current (DC)**, championed by Thomas Edison, or **alternating
current (AC)**, championed by George Westinghouse and powered by Nikola Tesla's inventions. It was equal parts
engineering contest and business war, complete with a genuinely ugly publicity campaign. AC won, and the
reason it won comes down to one humble device: the **transformer**.

Let's meet the three players first, then settle why one side won.


## The three players


### Thomas Edison — the DC incumbent
Edison got there first and bet everything on DC. In 1882 he opened the **Pearl Street Station** in lower
Manhattan, one of the world's first central power plants, sending **low-voltage DC** (about 110 volts) to
nearby customers. It worked — and Edison owned the patents, the equipment, and the business, so he had every
financial reason to defend it.

DC's fatal limitation: at low voltage you can only send power a short distance before the wires waste it as
heat. Edison's plants could economically serve only about a **one-mile radius**, which meant a power station
on nearly every block. That was fine for dense city centers and hopeless for everywhere else.

When AC threatened that empire, Edison fought dirty. He waged a **fear campaign** to paint AC as a deadly
menace: his associates publicly electrocuted stray animals with AC, he promoted the use of AC for the first
**electric chair** (the execution of William Kemmler in 1890), and supporters pushed the verb
"**Westinghoused**" for being killed by it. It was marketing by terror — and it ultimately failed.


### Nikola Tesla — the AC inventor
Tesla supplied the missing piece of physics. Briefly an Edison employee (he left after a falling-out), Tesla
went on to invent the **polyphase AC system** and, crucially, a practical **AC induction motor** built around
a clever idea: a **rotating magnetic field** that spins a motor with no fragile sparking brushes. Until then,
AC's big weakness was that nobody had a good AC motor; Tesla erased that weakness.

In 1888 Tesla **sold his AC patents to George Westinghouse**, marrying his inventions to the one company with
the capital and nerve to deploy them nationwide.


### George Westinghouse — the AC champion
Westinghouse was the businessman-engineer who turned AC into an industry. He bought Tesla's patents, hired
talented engineers like **William Stanley** (who built an early practical **transformer**), and staked his
company on the AC bet. Where Edison defended an installed base, Westinghouse pushed the technology that could
reach the whole country — and he was willing to take the financial risk to prove it.


## Why AC won: the transformer changes voltage; DC couldn't
Strip away the personalities and one technical fact decides the war. **Power lost as heat in a wire is I²R** —
it grows with the *square* of the current. So to ship power efficiently over long distances, you want **low
current**, which (since power = volts × amps) means **high voltage**.

But people can't use 100,000 volts at home — it's lethal and useless for a light bulb. So the winning system
needs to do two opposite things: **high voltage for the long trip, low voltage at the house.** That requires a
device that can change AC voltage up and down cheaply and reliably — the **transformer**, which works *only*
with alternating current.

That gave AC a decisive three-step recipe DC simply could not match in that era:

1. **Step up** the voltage at the power plant (low current → tiny losses over long wires).
2. **Transmit** it hundreds of miles on thin, cheap wire.
3. **Step down** the voltage near homes and factories to a safe, usable level.

DC of the 1880s had **no practical way to change voltage**, so it was stuck at the same low voltage from
generator to lamp — and therefore stuck within about a mile of the plant. AC + Tesla's motor + the transformer
could light a city *and* a distant farm from one big, efficient generating station. That's the whole ballgame:
**AC scaled, DC didn't.**


## The two knockout blows
Two public demonstrations turned the technical advantage into a decisive win:

1. **The 1893 Chicago World's Fair (World's Columbian Exposition).** Westinghouse underbid Edison's General
   Electric to light the entire fair with AC, illuminating it with some 100,000 lamps before millions of
   visitors. It was an unmissable, dazzling proof that AC was safe, practical, and ready for scale.
2. **The 1895 Niagara Falls hydroelectric plant.** Westinghouse built the generators (on Tesla's polyphase
   design) and, in 1896, transmitted AC power roughly **26 miles to Buffalo, New York** — a feat flatly
   impossible for Edison's DC. Long-distance power transmission was now a proven reality, and the argument
   was over.

The irony coda: even Edison's own company moved on. After mergers formed **General Electric** in 1892, GE
itself shifted to building AC equipment. AC at roughly **60 hertz** became the North American standard it
remains today.


## Bottom line
Edison built the first working electrical system and then fought — at times shamefully — to protect his
**DC** empire, which couldn't reach beyond about a mile. **Tesla** invented the AC induction motor and
polyphase system that made AC genuinely practical, then sold those patents to **Westinghouse**, who had the
capital and courage to deploy them. Together they won because **AC can be transformed to high voltage for
efficient long-distance transmission and back down to safe levels at the wall — and DC of that era could
not.** The 1893 Chicago Fair and the 1895 Niagara Falls project proved it to the world. Edison was first;
Westinghouse and Tesla were *right*. For more, see the [War of the Currents][01], [Nikola Tesla][02],
[George Westinghouse][03], and the [Adams Power Plant at Niagara Falls][04].
